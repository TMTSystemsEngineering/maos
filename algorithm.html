Physical Optics Beam Propagation

Maxwell Equation

The Maxwell equation predicts that

$$^2 {E} = {^2 {E}}{ t^2} $$ where $ = 1/c_0^2$

It has a general solution of the form

$${E}={E_0}f({{k}}{{x}} - c_0 t)$$ where ${{k}}$ is the wave propagation direction. We have ${E}{{k}}=0$ and $c_0{B}={{k}} {E}$.

Fresnel diffraction integral

The electric field at a point $(x,y,z)$ following an aperture at $z=0$ is given by

$$ E(x,y,z)={1}{i} E(x^, y^, 0){e^{ikr}}{r} {z}{r} dx^ dy^ $$

where $k=2/$ is the wave number and $r={(x-x^)^2+(y-y^)^2+z^2}$. This is the exact solution, but lacks analytical or convenient numerical solution.

Fresnel approximation

Introduce $^2=(x-x^)^2+(y-y^)^2$, and substitute $r$ with its Taylor expansion: $$r={^2+z^2}=z{1+{^2}{z^2}}=z+{^2}{2z}-{^4}{8z^3}+...$$ The equation can be simplified if we can ignore the third term, which requires that the phase $k{^4}{8z^3}$ or $$z^3{^4}{8}.$$ For the $r$ outside of the phase, we make another approximate by keeping only the first term, which is valid if the aperture is significantly smaller than the propagation distance: $ z$. The electric field can then be simplified as

$$E(x,y,z)={e^{ikz}}{i z} E(x^, y^,0){e^{{i}{z}[(x-x^)^2+(y-y^)^2]}} dx^ dy^$$

This is the Fresnel diffraction integral. It means that, if the Fresnel approximation is valid, the propagating field is a spherical wave, originating at the aperture and moving along z. The term before the integral represents constant phase evolution and energy normalization.

Angular Spectrum

The integral can be viewed as a convolution between $E(x,y,0)$ and $$h(x,y)=e^{{i}{z}(x^2+y^2)}.$$

The convolution can be done with Fourier transform (there is no need for embedding): $$E={F}^{-1}[{F}[E(x,y,0)]{F}[h(x,y)]].$$

The fourier transform of $h(x,y)$ can be computed analytically. Recall the Fourier transform $${F}[[ia(x^2+y^2)]]={i}{a}[{-i^2}{a}(f_x^2+f_y^2)]$$ for $k>0$. We have $${F}[h(x,y)]=iz[-iz(f_x^2+f_y^2)].$$ Using this requires upsampling since FFT convolution assumes the function is periodic. We do not use this method.

This convolution view is commonly referred to as the angular spectrum method. The original field $E(x,y,0)$ is only FFT'ed once. For each propagation distance, the FFT of $h()$ is multiplied to ${E}$ and then inverse FFT'ed to obtain the field. This approach is mostly useful in very near field propagations when $x$ and $x^$ has the same sampling.
